Quant Finance Key Terms Explained

  • Black-Scholes: A math model used for pricing European-style options. The formula accounts for key factors such as the spot price, strike price, volatility, risk-free rate, and time to expiration. It's widely used in the options market and is based on the assumption of lognormal asset returns.
  • Monte Carlo Simulation: A statistical technique that uses random sampling to simulate a range of possible outcomes in the pricing of an option. By simulating many price paths, it approximates the price of options whose pricing models may be difficult to solve analytically.
  • Implied Volatility: The volatility value implied by the market price of an option. It's derived using an option pricing model, like Black-Scholes, by solving for the volatility that would result in a price matching the current market price of the option. Implied volatility helps to gauge market expectations of future volatility.
  • Spot Price: The current market price of the underlying asset or stock.
  • Strike Price: The price at which an option holder can buy or sell the underlying asset. The strike price, in combination with the spot price, helps determine whether an option is in-the-money (ITM) or out-of-the-money (OTM).
  • Time to Maturity: The time remaining until the option expires, usually expressed in years. The closer an option is to expiration, the more its price is affected by time decay.
  • Risk-Free Rate: The theoretical return on an investment with no risk. It's often represented by the yield on short-term government bonds, such as U.S. Treasury bills.
  • Vega: This measures an option's sensitivity to volatility. It shows how much an option's price will change with a 1% change in the implied volatility of the underlying asset.
  • Delta: This measures the sensitivity of an option's price to changes in the price of the underlying asset. It represents the rate of change of the option price for a unit change in the underlying asset price.
  • Theta: This represents the rate of change of an option's price with respect to the passage of time such as time decay.

How I created this Web App

I used HTML/CSS and Flask to process user input, make the design, and display resulting calculations for options prices based on 3 popular models used in quantitative finance. I implemented the Black-Scholes model and Monte Carlo simulations using Python's scientific libraries such as NumPy and SciPy. I also calculated implied volatility through a Newton-Raphson method for a more advanced feature:D
